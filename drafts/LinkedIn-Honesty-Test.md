Two years ago I assumed bad AI results were my fault. Now I regularly wonder if the model is actually doing what I asked or just producing something that looks like it did.

It is a ridiculous position. We verify AI with other AI.

Context windows are sold as fully usable. They are not. I call the drop-off “silent shear”: the information stays in the window while reliable use quietly declines.

Many techniques become reasoning theatre. The output looks like a tree or verification process, but the actual steps often never happened. A “panel of experts” can be one model generating voices and steering them toward convenient consensus. The name is not proof the behaviour occurred.

I test this simply. I ask models to classify techniques into only three categories:

Natively reliable. Needs scaffolding. Fabricated.

Then the real question: for anything marked fabricated, explain what you actually do instead.

Chain of Code holds up because execution cannot be faked. Step Back prompting often works because abstraction fits how models pay attention. ReAct needs real tools. Tree of Thoughts and Mixture of Experts usually need external structure; without it the labels describe formatting more than thinking.

Stop trusting the names. Instead of “use Tree of Thoughts,” ask for three candidates, define criteria, compare them, and pick the strongest.

AI does not need to be deceptive to mislead. It only needs to generate the convincing appearance of reasoning that never occurred. That distinction is becoming essential AI literacy.
